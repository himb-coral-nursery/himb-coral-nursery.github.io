The HIMB Coral Nursery is an active in-situ laboratory. Below are some of our past collaborations.

## Coral Growth Modules (CGMs)
<a id="cgms"></a>
As a part of the Rapid Resilient Reefs for Coastal Defense project (R3D) we grew coral fragments from 4 different coral morphologies on 4 differently shaped concrete base modules designed to change the angle at which coral fragments are outplanted. This allowed us to investigate how the angle of incident light impacts the larger colony morphology as fragments fuse and grow. The results of this experiment influenced the design and inclusion of a full-size, shelf-shaped CGM on the final large-scale wave nature-based breakwater.

## Long-Term Shading Influences *Montipora capitata* Colony Morphology
<a id="ducret"></a>
Shading both plating and branching growth forms of the coral *Montipora capitata* for 1 year revealed how differences in light cause colonies in the same environment to grow into sigfnificantly different forms while their symbiont community remains stable. This work was performed as a collaboration with Dr. Hugo Ducret from the Université libre de Bruxelles as a part of his PhD research.

## The Underwater Zooplankton Enhancement Array (UZELA)
<a id="uzela"></a>
Dr. Andrea Grottoli of Ohio State University ran experiments here at the HIMB Coral Nursery and in our tank system while developing UZELA. She writes:

Coral feeding on zooplankton increases as zooplankton concentrations increase. In addition, resilience is higher in corals that eat more zooplankton. To leverage the advantage that zooplankton feeding has on coral resilience, we developed the Underwater Zooplankton Light Enhancement Array (UZELA) to locally concentrate natural zooplankton and give corals more to eat. UZELA is programmable, autonomous, waterproof, and deployable for six months on a single battery. In the field, UZELA increases local zooplankton concentrations by 7-fold and increases coral feeding rates by 10 to 50-fold. Field tests on Hawaiian and Floridian corals shows that UZELA can enhance coral growth, survivorship, and health metrics. New advances in UZELA include a self-cleaning wiper, rechargeable batteries, and an easy-to-use software interface that gives end-users options in the frequency and timing of the illumination and wiper functions. Technological interventions like UZELA can be part of the solution for enhancing restoration success.
